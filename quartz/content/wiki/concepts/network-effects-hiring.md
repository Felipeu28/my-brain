# Network Effects in Hiring Platforms

**Type:** Concept — business model framework
**Last updated:** 2026-04-12
**Related:** [[wiki/concepts/two-sided-marketplace]], [[wiki/concepts/cost-per-hire]], [[wiki/radar/competitor-moves]]

---

## Summary

Network effects in hiring occur when the platform becomes more valuable as more users (employers and workers) join. This is the moat that makes dominant job boards (Indeed, LinkedIn) hard to displace — and the structural advantage Moil must deliberately build.

## Types of Network Effects in Hiring

### 1. Supply-Side Density (Direct)
More workers → better candidate pool → better matches → employers stay → more jobs → attracts more workers.

*Moil relevance*: Spanish-speaking blue-collar workers are underserved on current platforms. Early Moil concentration here creates a unique supply-side density that incumbents can't easily replicate.

### 2. Data Network Effects
More hiring data → better AI matching models → better candidate ranking → better outcomes → more trust from employers.

*Moil relevance*: Every Moil hire produces feedback data (did the worker show up? were they retained at 90 days?). Over 1,000+ hires, this becomes a matching advantage no new entrant can replicate.

### 3. Geographic Clustering
Hiring is hyper-local. A dense network in Austin doesn't help a Denver employer. This cuts both ways — it protects Moil's Austin position AND means expansion to each new market requires starting over.

### 4. Reputation / Word-of-Mouth (Informal Network Effects)
In the blue-collar worker community, informal networks are primary. Workers refer each other via WhatsApp groups, in-person at worksites, and family networks.

*Moil relevance*: Every satisfied worker placed by Moil is a potential recruiter. This is the most underinvested network effect — worth deliberately building (referral programs, worker reviews, community events).

## The Incumbent Moat Problem

Indeed and LinkedIn have massive network effects — but primarily for:
- English-speaking workers
- White-collar and formal-sector roles
- Workers who are ATS-native

Moil's bet is that these network effects **do not transfer** to Spanish-speaking, mobile-first, blue-collar workers who find jobs via WhatsApp groups — and that Moil can build a parallel graph in this segment.
